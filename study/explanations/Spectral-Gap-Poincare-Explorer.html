A Markov chain on 8 states. Mass starts at state 1 and converges to π (uniform). States 1–4 are Valley A, states 5–8 are Valley B — separated by a barrier. The spectral gap γ = 1 − λ₂ controls convergence: KL(pₖ ‖ π) ≤ (1−γ)ᵏ · KL(p₀ ‖ π). Raise the barrier → lower p_cross → smaller γ → exponentially slower mixing.

The Poincaré inequality: for any test function f on the state space, Var_π(f) ≤ (1/γ) · ε(f, f).
• Var_π(f) = how spread out f is at equilibrium (global variance)
• ε(f, f) = Dirichlet form = average squared change in f per step (local movement)
• If ε is small but Var is large, the chain makes tiny progress per step → γ must be small. The second eigenvector is the worst-case f — it maximises the ratio Var/ε = 1/γ.
A particle doing Metropolis–Hastings random walk on a double-well potential V(x). Barrier height controls how hard it is to cross. By the Arrhenius formula: γ ≈ exp(−2ΔV / T). High barrier + low temperature → exponentially rare crossings → γ ≈ 0. This is exactly the situation for Gibbs correctors on multimodal text distributions.
A Gibbs corrector picks a position d and resamples it. Uniform: all positions equally likely. Informed (β > 0): weight ∝ exp(β · surpriseᵈ). By the rearrangement inequality: putting more weight on high-surprise positions (which also have large local Dirichlet contributions) increases ε_informed ≥ ε_uniform, and therefore γ_informed ≥ γ_uniform. The gain grows with Var[surprise].
